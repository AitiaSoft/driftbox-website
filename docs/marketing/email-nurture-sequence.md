## Email 4: Research Insights

**Subject:** I asked 50 professionals about lost messages. Here's what surprised me.
**Send Timing:** Day 14
**Goal:** Share original research, build authority, create "I'm not alone" feeling

---

Hey {first_name},

Over the past few months, I've had in-depth conversations with 50 professionals about how they communicate at work. Product managers, engineers, founders, consultants, project managers, sales leaders — across companies from 5 people to 5,000.

I want to share what I learned, because some of it genuinely surprised me.

**Finding #1: The "I know I saw it somewhere" problem is universal.**
48 out of 50 people — 96% — told me they regularly remember seeing an important piece of information but can't remember which tool it was in. Was it Slack? Email? A Teams message? A comment on a Google Doc? The information exists, but finding it again takes 10-20 minutes. Or they just give up and ask the person to repeat themselves.

**Finding #2: Most people have developed private workarounds — and they feel guilty about it.**
37 out of 50 told me they maintain some kind of personal system to track important messages. Notebooks, personal Notion pages, pinned messages, screenshot folders, even sending themselves emails. They know it's inefficient. They do it anyway because nothing else works.

**Finding #3: "Tool fatigue" is worse than anyone admits publicly.**
29 out of 50 said they feel genuinely overwhelmed by the number of communication channels they're expected to monitor. But almost nobody talks about it at work because it feels like a personal failing — like they should be better at "keeping up."

**Finding #4: The real cost isn't time. It's trust.**
This one hit me the hardest. 22 out of 50 described situations where a missed message led to a real trust breakdown — a client who felt ignored, a teammate who thought they were being blown off, a deadline that slipped because a handoff got lost. The communication failure created a people problem.

**Finding #5: People don't want another app. They want a layer.**
When I asked "What would actually help?", the most common answer was some version of: "Something that works across everything I already use without me having to change my habits." Not a new inbox. Not another dashboard to check. A layer that catches what falls through the cracks.

That last finding is essentially the DriftBox product thesis in one sentence. We're not building tool #12 for your toolbar. We're building the intelligence layer that sits across tools 1 through 11 and makes sure nothing important gets lost.

I'll share more about how that actually works in my next email.

For now, I'm curious — did any of these findings resonate with you? Which one hit closest to home? Reply and let me know.

Roberto

P.S. — If you filled out the communication audit from my last email, thank you. I've been reading the responses and they're reinforcing everything I'm seeing in these conversations.

## Email 5: Product Walkthrough

**Subject:** Here's how DriftBox will actually work
**Send Timing:** Day 21
**Goal:** Make the product tangible, address "is this real?" skepticism, build anticipation

---

Hey {first_name},

You've been hearing me talk about the problem for three weeks. Today I want to show you the solution.

Here's how DriftBox will actually work when you get access.

**Step 1: Connect your tools (2 minutes)**
You'll link DriftBox to the communication tools you already use. Slack, Microsoft Teams, Gmail, Outlook — we're starting with the big four and expanding from there. It's a standard OAuth connection, the same way you connect apps to Google or Microsoft today. DriftBox gets read access. It never sends messages on your behalf.

**Step 2: DriftBox starts listening — intelligently.**
Once connected, our AI begins processing your communication streams. But it's not recording everything or building a surveillance log. It's looking for specific patterns: decisions being made, action items being assigned, deadlines being set, questions going unanswered, and important information that's at risk of being lost.

**Step 3: Your Daily Brief.**
Every morning (or whenever you choose), DriftBox delivers a brief — a concise summary of what matters across all your channels. Think of it like a chief of staff who read everything overnight and is giving you the 3-minute version. Decisions that were made. Things that need your attention. Action items assigned to you or your team. Follow-ups that are overdue.

**Step 4: Smart Search.**
When you need to find something, you search once and DriftBox searches everywhere. "What did the client say about the Q3 timeline?" — instead of opening Slack, then email, then Teams, you ask DriftBox and get the answer with a link to the original message, regardless of which platform it lives on.

**Step 5: Drift Alerts.**
This is the feature I'm most excited about. DriftBox detects when something important is "drifting" — a decision that was made but never followed up on, an action item that's going stale, a client question that's been sitting unanswered for too long. You get a gentle nudge before it becomes a problem.

**What it won't do:**
DriftBox won't replace any of your tools. It won't add another chat window. It won't require your team to change how they communicate. It works in the background, like a safety net for your team's information flow.

**Privacy and security:**
Your data is encrypted in transit and at rest. We use enterprise-grade infrastructure (built on Supabase and Vercel). DriftBox processes your messages to extract insights but doesn't store raw message content long-term. You control exactly which channels are connected, and you can disconnect at any time.

**Pricing (planned):**
We're targeting $29/month per user for the full platform. Early waitlist members will get a significant discount — I'll share details when we're closer to launch.

This is real, it's being built right now, and the people on this waitlist are going to be the first to use it.

Questions? Reply anytime. I answer every email.
